1  Notation and Conventions

This chapter provides a comprehensive reference for every symbol, index, parameter, matrix, and derived quantity used in the theory of structural equivalence between Bayesian meta-analysis and Bayesian survey inference. All notation is defined once here and used consistently throughout subsequent chapters. Where the meta-analytic and survey literatures use different names for the same mathematical object, the unified symbol is given alongside both field-specific terms.

1.1 Index System

The theory operates over a two-level clustered data structure. Four index variables appear throughout.

NoteTerminological Convention

Throughout this book, the terms “study” (meta-analysis), “PSU” or “cluster” (survey), and “group” (general) are used interchangeably to denote the level-2 unit indexed by \(j\). Similarly, “effect size” (meta-analysis) and “observation” (survey) both refer to the level-1 unit indexed by \(i\) within \(j\).

NoteConvention on Cluster Size

Throughout the theory, we distinguish three regimes:

  1. Balanced designs: \(n_j = n\) for all \(j\), so \(N = nJ\) and \(\bar{n} = n\). Closed-form results (e.g., the conservation law) are stated in this regime.
  2. Unbalanced designs: \(n_j\) varies across \(j\). Formulas involving averages (e.g., \(\overline{\mathrm{DER}_\theta}\)) require careful treatment due to Jensen’s inequality effects.
  3. Singleton clusters: \(n_j = 1\) for all \(j\). This reduces to the univariate random-effects meta-analytic model (Fay–Herriot model in the survey context). Compound symmetry becomes vacuous, and \(\mathrm{DEFF}_j = 1\).

1.2.2 Variance Components

ImportantNotation Convention: \(\sigma^2_\theta\) vs. \(\tau^2\)

The meta-analytic literature almost universally uses \(\tau^2\) for between-study heterogeneity. We use \(\sigma^2_\theta\) throughout to (i) maintain notational consistency with the survey literature, (ii) avoid collision with the DER classification threshold notation, and (iii) make the parametric role transparent: \(\sigma^2_\theta\) is the variance of the random effects \(\theta_j\). The correspondence is \(\sigma^2_\theta \equiv \tau^2\).

1.3.2 Sampling Variances and Error Variances

ImportantKey Correction: Cluster-Specific Variance Identification

The exactness condition (E2) requires cluster-specific identification \(\sigma^2_{e,j} = \bar{s}_j^2\), not a single global \(\sigma^2_e\) matched to all studies simultaneously. When the within-study average sampling variances \(\bar{s}_j^2\) vary across studies (which is the typical case in meta-analysis), exact isomorphism demands that the survey error variance be matched study-by-study. If a common \(\sigma^2_e\) is used on the survey side while \(\bar{s}_j^2\) varies across \(j\) on the meta-analytic side, the quadratic forms in the two likelihoods differ by a \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\)-dependent term, breaking exact isomorphism. See Chapter 3 for full details.

1.3.3 Predictor Variables

1.3.4 Survey Design Quantities

NoteConvention: Weights in Meta-Analysis

In standard meta-analysis, no design weights appear: each effect size enters the likelihood with implicit unit weight. Under exactness condition (E3), \(\tilde{w}_{ij} = 1\) for all \((i,j)\), aligning the survey pseudo-likelihood with the standard meta-analytic likelihood. When (E3) is violated, the two frameworks target different estimands.

1.3.5 Correlation Parameters

NoteConvention: Study-Specific Correlation and DEFF

These quantities should not be conflated: \(\rho\) is the analyst-chosen working correlation inside \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{\mathrm{work}}(\rho)\), whereas \(\rho_j^{\mathrm{CE}}\) and \(\rho_j^{\mathrm{CHE}}\) are model-implied marginal ICCs obtained after integrating over the random effects.

The quantities \(\rho_j^{\mathrm{CE}} = \sigma^2_\theta / (\bar{s}_j^2 + \sigma^2_\theta)\) and \(\rho_j^{\mathrm{CHE}} = \sigma^2_\theta / (\bar{s}_j^2 + \omega^2 + \sigma^2_\theta)\) are study-specific because \(\bar{s}_j^2\) varies across studies. Similarly, the study-specific DEFF is \(\mathrm{DEFF}_j = 1 + (n_j - 1)\rho_j\). When the context is clear or when discussing average/typical values, we sometimes drop the \(j\) subscript and write \(\rho^{\mathrm{CE}}\), \(\rho^{\mathrm{CHE}}\), and \(\mathrm{DEFF}\) without ambiguity.

1.4.3 Sandwich Components

ImportantConvention: Likelihood-Only Scores in the Meat

The meat matrix \(\mathbf{M}\) always uses likelihood-only cluster scores \(\mathbf{s}_j(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \ell_j(\boldsymbol{\phi})\), not posterior scores that include prior contributions. The bread may optionally use the posterior Hessian \(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{post}} = \mathbf{H} + \mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{prior}}\), following Williams and Savitsky (2021). This distinction is critical for correct implementation of both the sandwich variance and the Cholesky correction.

1.4.4 Cholesky Factors

1.4.5 Hessian Block Structure

For the combined parameter \(\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\boldsymbol{\beta}^{\intercal}, \boldsymbol{\theta}^{\intercal})^{\intercal}\), the Hessian has a \(2 \times 2\) block partition:

\[ \mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\boldsymbol{\beta}} & \mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\boldsymbol{\theta}} \\ \mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\boldsymbol{\beta}} & \mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\boldsymbol{\theta}} \end{pmatrix}, \]

where \(\mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) is \(p \times p\), \(\mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\boldsymbol{\theta}}\) is \(J \times J\), and \(\mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\boldsymbol{\theta}}\) is \(p \times J\). The Schur complement with respect to the random effects is:

\[ \mathbf{S}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\boldsymbol{\beta}} - \mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\boldsymbol{\theta}}\,\mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\boldsymbol{\theta}}^{-1}\,\mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\boldsymbol{\beta}}. \]

This Schur complement governs identifiability (regularity condition C7) and the fixed-effect DER decomposition.

1.5 Derived Quantities: DER Framework

1.5.1 Design Effect and Design Effect Ratio

NoteThree-Regime Interpretation of DER
  • \(\mathrm{DER}_k = 1\): The working-model posterior is calibrated; no correction needed.
  • \(\mathrm{DER}_k > 1\): The posterior is under-dispersed (credible intervals too narrow); the working model understates uncertainty.
  • \(\mathrm{DER}_k < 1\): The posterior is over-dispersed (credible intervals too wide); the working model overstates uncertainty.

1.5.2 Shrinkage and Information Quantities

NoteConvention on \(R_k\) and \(\gamma_k\)

For theorem-level statements, \(R_k\) and \(\gamma_k\) should be understood as information-fraction quantities derived from the Schur complement of the posterior Hessian, not merely raw variance ratios. The variance-ratio formulas given above are exact under balanced designs with orthogonal covariates. In general unbalanced meta-regression, the information-based definition is:

\[ R_k = \frac{[\mathbf{S}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{\mathrm{post}\,-1}]_{kk} - [\mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{\mathrm{post}\,-1}]_{kk}}{[\mathbf{S}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{\mathrm{post}\,-1}]_{kk}}, \]

where \(\mathbf{S}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{\mathrm{post}}\) is the Schur complement of the posterior Hessian \(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{post}}\) with respect to the random effects. Under a flat \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) prior (\(h_\mu = 0\)), \(R_\mu = B\) exactly for the intercept in balanced designs. Under a proper \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) prior (\(h_\mu > 0\)), \(R_\mu = J\alpha B / (J\alpha + h_\mu)\), which is asymptotically exact (\(R_\mu \to B\)) under condition (C6) (\(J\alpha \gg h_\mu\)). The relationship \(R_k \approx \gamma_k B\) is a balanced-design heuristic; the Schur-complement definition above is the canonical one in general.

This covariance-based definition is the canonical definition used throughout the theory. It is the only version that is sign-correct (yielding \(R_k \in [0, B]\)) and generalizes properly to unbalanced multivariate settings. Other formulations (e.g., using raw information blocks \([\mathbf{S}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{\mathrm{post}}]_{kk}/[\mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{\mathrm{post}}]_{kk}\), or products of inverse and non-inverse blocks) may fail to preserve the correct sign or reduce to \(B\) in the balanced scalar case. See Definition 7 in Chapter 7 for the formal definition.

1.7 Likelihood and Posterior Notation

1.8 Isomorphism Map

The isomorphism map \(\mathcal{T}\) sends each mathematical object in the survey framework to its meta-analytic counterpart (the full proof is given in Chapter 4, Theorem 1). The two core identifications are:

\[ \mathcal{T}: \quad \sigma^2_{e,j} \;\mapsto\; \bar{s}_j^2, \qquad \tilde{w}_{ij} \;\mapsto\; 1. \]

Under \(\mathcal{T}\), the survey pseudo-log-likelihood and the meta-analytic log-likelihood differ by a constant \(c\) that does not depend on \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\):

\[ \ell^{(w)}_{\mathrm{surv}}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = \ell_{\mathrm{meta}}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) + c. \]

The constant \(c\) depends on \(\{\sigma^2_{e,j}\}\), \(\{\bar{s}_j^2\}\), \(\{\tilde{w}_{ij}\}\), \(\rho\), and \(\{n_j\}\), but not on \(\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\boldsymbol{\beta}^{\intercal}, \boldsymbol{\theta}^{\intercal})^{\intercal}\).

NoteNote on \(\mathcal{T}\) and Condition (E4)

The isomorphism map \(\mathcal{T}\) has two core identifications, corresponding to conditions (E2) and (E3). Condition (E4) — that all predictors are study-level (\(\mathbf{x}_{ij} = \mathbf{x}_j\)) — is not part of the isomorphism. It is a DER simplification condition needed only for the closed-form between-direction result \(R_k = B\) in Theorem 3. When (E4) is relaxed, the isomorphism still holds under (E1), (E2), (E3), and (E5) alone. Outside the between direction, summaries such as \(\mathrm{DEFF}(1-\gamma_k B)\) should be treated as heuristic rather than theorem-level identities.

1.11 Typographic and Mathematical Conventions

  1. Boldface: Vectors (\(\boldsymbol{\beta}\), \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\), \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\), \(\mathbf{y}_j\), \(\mathbf{r}_j\), \(\mathbf{s}_j\)) and matrices (\(\mathbf{H}\), \(\mathbf{M}\), \(\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{sand}}\), \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\), \(\mathbf{X}_j\)) are set in boldface.
  2. Scalars: Scalar quantities (\(y_{ij}\), \(\theta_j\), \(\sigma^2_\theta\), \(\rho\), \(B_j\)) are set in italic.
  3. Subscripts: The subscript convention \(j\) (cluster), \(i\) (within-cluster observation), \(k\) (coefficient), \(s\) (MCMC draw) is used consistently.
  4. Superscripts: Framework labels appear as superscripts: \(\ell^{(w)}_{\mathrm{surv}}\), \(\ell_{\mathrm{meta}}\), \(\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{sand}}^{\mathrm{surv}}\), \(\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{sand}}^{\mathrm{meta}}\).
  5. Transpose: The transpose of a vector or matrix is denoted \((\cdot)^{\intercal}\).
  6. Cholesky: \(\mathrm{chol}(\mathbf{A})\) denotes the lower-triangular Cholesky factor of a positive-definite matrix \(\mathbf{A}\).
  7. Gradient and Hessian: \(\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}\) denotes the gradient operator; \(\nabla^2_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}\) denotes the Hessian operator.
  8. Positive definiteness: \(\mathbf{A} \succ 0\) means \(\mathbf{A}\) is positive definite.
  9. Convergence in distribution: \(\overset{d}{\longrightarrow}\) denotes convergence in distribution as \(J \to \infty\).
  10. End of proof: \(\blacksquare\) marks the end of a proof.
